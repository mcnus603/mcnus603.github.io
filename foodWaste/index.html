Change in TNS Dining Creates Oppoortunity to Improve Food Waste

by SARAH MCNUTT

Students carry their Murray’s Bagels, their Merci Market salads and takeout containers from New School Cafeteria as they pass between buildings. They snack in their classes and take dinner breaks in studios, but in most of the buildings their half eaten meals will end up in the landfill.

There are few locations for students to dispose food waste since only two of the 18 New School buildings collect compost, according to sustainability reports.

However, the New School has an opportunity to improve their food waste system after announcing in March that they will not renew their contract with Chartwells, their food services provide. Instead, they plan to move operations directly in-house. (redundant)

“We need to really think about how we handle sustainability when we transition to this new system,” said Erik Eibert, director of sustainable initiatives about this change (do I need this). “It’s good in some ways because we have a little bit more control over how things happen.”

Stephen Stabile, vice president of business and finance and treasurer explained that this change will give the dinning services more flexibility when it comes to food options and managing sustainability.

We want to be able to make some of the changes or do some of the things that we probably haven’t even thought of yet in making it a dining program that is adaptable to what students are looking for now, can it be even more sustainable, all of those things that we stand for,” Stabile said. “Chartwells wasn’t really able to do a lot of that for us. That is the bottom line.”

Although President David Van Zandt has guaranteed jobs to Chartwells members in a university-wide, Stabile said there will be positions that require data analytic skills to help the university not overproduce.

“We’ll be able to on more granular level, look at student prefences and so on, and the patterns in which things get consumed throughout the day. Really watching that in real-time is one of our strategies around that,” said Ed Verdi Senior Director of Business Operations.

All of the food waste at the New School goes to compost where it is converted to biogas and fertilzarer.

“Those promoting biogas are arguing that if you’re trying to create a system where you’re using renewable fuels, ethanol is the answer”. said Andrew Smith, culinary historian and faculty in the New School’s Food Studies Program. “Obviously it’s better not to have produced the food in the first place but if you’re going to produce the food you need to explore it in new ways like biogas.”
